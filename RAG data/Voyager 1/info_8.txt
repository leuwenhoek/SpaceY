Origins of the Voyager Mission and the Grand Tour

The concept of the Voyager mission began in the mid-1960s at NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory. Gary Flandro, an aerospace engineer, calculated that a rare alignment of the outer planets—Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune, and Pluto—would occur in the late 1970s. This alignment, which happens only once every 175 years, would allow a single spacecraft to visit all of these planets by using gravity assists to swing from one planet to the next, significantly reducing the travel time and fuel required.

NASA originally planned an ambitious "Planetary Grand Tour" project that would launch four highly complex spacecraft in the late 1970s to explore the outer solar system. However, in December 1971, the U.S. government canceled the Grand Tour due to funding constraints and redirected resources to the Space Shuttle program. In response, NASA scaled down the project, proposing a more modest mission to launch two Mariner-derived probes to Jupiter and Saturn, which ultimately became the Voyager program.
