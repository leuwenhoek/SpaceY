Voyager Spacecraft Structure and Engineering Design

The Voyager spacecraft are designed to survive the harsh environment of deep space for decades. At launch, each spacecraft weighed approximately 815 kilograms (1,797 pounds), which included a scientific payload of 105 kilograms (231 pounds). After decades of consuming hydrazine propellant, the mass of each probe is now about 733 kilograms (1,616 pounds). The central body of the spacecraft is a decagonal prism bus that houses the electronics, computers, and propellant tanks.

To maintain their orientation in space, the Voyagers use a three-axis stabilization system. This system relies on gyroscopes, accelerometers, a Sun sensor, and a Canopus star tracker to monitor the spacecraft's attitude. The onboard computers process this data and fire small hydrazine thrusters to keep the 3.7-meter (12-foot) high-gain antenna pointed precisely toward Earth. The scientific instruments are mounted on booms to isolate them from the magnetic fields of the spacecraft, and the cameras and spectrometers are placed on a steerable scan platform to allow precise targeting.
