The Pale Blue Dot and the Family Portrait

On February 14, 1990, as Voyager 1 was leaving the solar system and approaching a distance of 6 billion kilometers (3.7 billion miles, or 40.5 astronomical units) from Earth, NASA engineers commanded the spacecraft to turn its camera back toward the planets. The resulting sequence of images, known as the "Family Portrait," captured six of the solar system's planets: Neptune, Uranus, Saturn, Jupiter, Earth, and Venus.

Among these images was the famous "Pale Blue Dot," which shows Earth as a tiny, pixel-sized blue speck suspended in a scattered beam of sunlight. The photograph was taken at the request of Carl Sagan, who used it as the centerpiece of his book reflecting on humanity's place in the universe. Sagan famously noted that the image underscores our responsibility to deal more kindly with one another, and to preserve and cherish the pale blue dot, the only home we've ever known.
