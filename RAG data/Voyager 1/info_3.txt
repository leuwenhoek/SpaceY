Voyager 1's Jupiter Encounter

Voyager 1 began its scientific observations of Jupiter in April 1978, when it was still 165 million miles (265 million kilometers) away from the giant planet. As the spacecraft neared its target, the images it returned revealed a highly turbulent and dynamic Jovian atmosphere, displaying far more violent storm activity than had been observed during the earlier Pioneer 10 and 11 flybys. To document this active atmosphere, the spacecraft took high-resolution photographs every 96 seconds, which engineers later assembled into a historic time-lapse movie showing the complex motions of Jupiter's colorful cloud bands.

The spacecraft made its closest approach to Jupiter on March 5, 1979, flying at a distance of approximately 217,000 miles (349,000 kilometers) from the planet's cloud tops. Perhaps the most spectacular discovery of the Jupiter flyby was the detection of active volcanism on the moon Io. Voyager 1 captured images of massive volcanic plumes erupting miles into space, providing the first direct evidence of active volcanoes on another body in our solar system. In addition to Io, Voyager 1 provided close-up images of the Galilean moons Ganymede, Callisto, and Europa, and discovered a thin, previously unseen ring system surrounding Jupiter, along with two small Jovian moons, Adrastea and Metis.
