Voyager 1's Saturn Encounter

On November 12, 1980, Voyager 1 made its closest approach to Saturn, flying within approximately 78,000 miles (126,000 kilometers) of the planet's cloud tops. The encounter was as spectacular and scientifically rich as the Jupiter flyby. The probe's cameras captured stunning images of Saturn's intricate ring system, revealing complex features such as dark, radial "spokes" in the B-ring and thin, braided structures. The spacecraft also identified "shepherd moons"—small satellites like Prometheus and Pandora whose gravity keeps the edges of the F-ring sharply defined.

During the encounter, Voyager 1 photographed several of Saturn's ice-covered moons, including Mimas, Enceladus, Tethys, Dione, and Rhea. The close-up images revealed that these moons are composed largely of water ice and feature diverse terrains, ranging from heavily cratered surfaces to smooth plains that suggest past geologic activity. Voyager 1 also discovered five new moons orbiting Saturn and detected a faint, outer ring structure that was named the G-ring.
